## How to use this document
Before making any decision about UI, copy, onboarding, CTAs, feature naming, upgrade prompts, guest mode, or product messaging — read the relevant section of this document first.
This is not a brand guidelines PDF. It is a working decision-making tool.
If a proposed change conflicts with anything in this document, the document wins unless there is a deliberate, reasoned decision to update it.

## Decision Filter
Before implementing any feature, ask:

- Does this increase confidence or just activity?
- Does this make the workflow clearer or noisier?
- Would a senior professional feel comfortable using this publicly?
- Does this feel editorial or "AI tool"?
- Does this reduce friction without reducing quality?
- Is this helping the user think better or just produce faster?
- Would this still feel premium in 3 years?

If the answer to multiple questions is "no", the feature should be reconsidered.

## Visual Identity Philosophy
LucentSignal should feel:

- Editorial, not corporate
- Deliberate, not energetic
- Premium, not luxurious
- Intelligent, not technical
- Modern, not futuristic

The interface should resemble:

- A high-end publishing environment
- A creative editorial workspace
- A premium productivity tool built for adults

It should NOT resemble:

- Crypto products
- Growth hacking software
- Social media dashboards
- Productivity gamification tools
- AI "magic" products

## Motion Principles
Animation should communicate state, not entertain.
Rules:

- Fast transitions
- Minimal movement
- No bouncing
- No celebratory animations
- No attention-seeking motion
- No animated gradients
- No loading theatrics

Motion should feel calm, intelligent, and restrained.

## Scoring Integrity
The scoring system must prioritise honesty over encouragement.
A weak post should receive a weak score.
Users must trust the scoring system more than they trust generic AI output.
Inflated scores destroy trust permanently.

## Feature Creep Rules
LucentSignal should expand depth-first, not breadth-first.
New features must strengthen:

- Idea quality
- Refinement
- Confidence
- Workflow continuity
- Publishing consistency

Avoid:

- Analytics dashboards
- Vanity metrics
- Engagement gamification
- "AI assistants"

The product should remain tightly focused on professional publishing workflow.

## 1. Brand Positioning System

### What LucentSignal is
LucentSignal is a professional publishing workflow for experienced people who already have ideas worth sharing but struggle to consistently turn rough thinking into publishable LinkedIn content.
The product covers the full arc from rough idea to scheduled post: generation, scoring, refinement, and scheduling. It is not a single-step tool. It is a workflow system with a clear beginning, middle, and end.

### What LucentSignal is not

- Not an AI writer
- Not a content generator
- Not a LinkedIn hack tool
- Not a prompt wrapper
- Not a productivity app
- Not a writing assistant
- Not a social media management platform
- Not a content calendar tool
- Not for people who want to post more — for people who want to post better

### Category definition
LucentSignal occupies the category of professional content workflow — not AI tooling, not social media management, not copywriting software.
The nearest honest comparison is not Jasper or Copy.ai. It is the discipline a professional journalist or communications director brings to turning a rough thought into something publishable. LucentSignal systematises that discipline.

### Emotional positioning
Users arrive with one of three emotional states:

- Frustrated — they know they should be posting, they have ideas, but nothing ever reaches the publish button
- Embarrassed — they have posted before and felt the output was generic, shallow, or not representative of their actual thinking
- Avoidant — they tell themselves they are too busy, but the real blocker is uncertainty about whether what they produce will reflect well on them

LucentSignal's emotional promise is not "post more content." It is: "What you produce will be worth publishing."
That is a fundamentally different emotional register. It addresses quality anxiety, not time anxiety.

### Behavioural positioning
The user's core behaviour we are enabling is not content creation. It is consistent, high-quality public thinking.
The blocker is not capability — our users are senior, experienced, articulate. The blocker is the gap between the quality of their thinking and the quality of their output. LucentSignal closes that gap.

### Premium positioning
LucentSignal is priced and positioned for professionals who take their reputation seriously. The product must at all times feel like it was built by people who also take quality seriously.
Premium positioning means:

- Restraint over noise
- Clarity over cleverness
- Confidence over persuasion
- Workflow over magic

### Anti-positioning rules
These are permanent. They are not negotiable for any marketing, product, or UX decision.

- Never position as "AI-powered" — position as workflow-driven
- Never position as fast — position as considered
- Never position around volume — position around quality
- Never position against competitors — position around the user's own standard
- Never use transformation language — the user is already good, not being transformed
- Never imply the product replaces thinking — it supports it

## 2. Core Product Narrative

### The transformation
Before: The user has an idea. It stays in their head, or becomes a draft they are never happy with, or gets posted in a form they regret.
After: The user has an idea. It becomes a draft worth reading, scored against real quality dimensions, refined to the standard they actually want, and scheduled when they want it to go out.
The transformation is not "you become a content creator." It is "your thinking finally makes it out of your head and into the world in a form you are proud of."

### The workflow story
LucentSignal has four stages. Every product decision should reinforce this arc:

1. Idea — rough topic, no pressure to be polished
2. Draft — generated from the idea, in the user's tone and style
3. Score and refine — four-dimension scoring, specific improvement guidance, human editing
4. Schedule — set a date, export to platform, done

This is a workflow, not a one-click tool. The four stages should be visible, named consistently, and felt by the user as progress — not as friction.

### The publishing psychology
Most experienced professionals do not post consistently because of one unspoken fear: the gap between what they know and what they produce.
They are articulate in meetings. They are sharp in written communication. But a LinkedIn post somehow exposes them to a different kind of judgement — and they know when the output does not represent their actual thinking.
LucentSignal addresses this directly. The scoring system is not a gimmick. It is a professional quality check that tells the user: this is ready to publish, or here is what would make it stronger.

### Why existing AI tools fail
Generic AI tools fail senior professionals for three reasons:

1. They sound like AI. The output is fluent but hollow. The user knows it. Their audience knows it.
2. They have no quality standard. There is no signal telling the user whether what was generated is actually good.
3. They have no continuity. Every generation starts from zero. There is no workflow, no history, no voice.

LucentSignal addresses all three: anti-template generation, four-dimension scoring, and persistent voice profile.

### Why workflow matters more than generation
A generated post is worth nothing if the user does not publish it. The publishing bottleneck is not the draft — it is confidence in the draft.
The scoring system, refinement tools, and editing workflow exist to build that confidence systematically. Generation is step one. The workflow is the product.

## 3. Audience Psychology

### Who the users really are

- Founders and senior operators who have genuine expertise but inconsistent publishing habits
- Consultants and advisers who know their LinkedIn presence should reflect their professional standing but rarely have time to make it happen
- Agency strategists and marketing directors who produce excellent work for clients but underinvest in their own voice
- Senior B2B professionals who have seen too much generic AI content and refuse to contribute to it
- People who are already good communicators but find the LinkedIn format awkward or reductive

These are not junior marketers learning to write. They are experienced people who already know what good looks like.

### What frustrates them

- Producing something that sounds like everyone else
- Feeling like AI tools make them sound less like themselves, not more
- Starting a draft and abandoning it halfway because it did not feel right
- Seeing junior colleagues post confidently while they overthink every word
- Spending 45 minutes on a post and still not publishing it
- Knowing they have valuable things to say but not knowing how to say them in this format

### Why they hesitate to post
The hesitation is almost never about time. It is about:

- Standard — "This does not represent what I actually think"
- Authenticity — "This sounds like an AI wrote it"
- Exposure — "My peers will read this and it needs to be right"
- Consistency — "I post once and then nothing for three months — it looks worse than not posting"

### Why they hesitate to pay

- They have been burned by AI tools that did not deliver professional-quality output
- They are sceptical that a workflow tool will survive contact with their actual use case
- They do not want a monthly commitment for something they will use twice
- They need to see that the output quality is genuinely different before they commit

This is why the free trial must deliver a full workflow experience — not just a single generated post.

### What makes them distrust AI products

- Language that sounds like the tool, not like them
- Output they would be embarrassed to publish
- Promises about speed and volume rather than quality
- Aggressive upgrade prompts that feel transactional
- Generic SaaS design that does not feel built for professionals
- Marketing copy that uses the same clichés the tool is supposed to help them avoid

### What emotional state they should leave in
Not excited. Not impressed by technology. Confident.
The user should leave thinking: "That is something I would actually publish." That confidence is the product.

## 4. Tone of Voice System

### Sentence structure
Short to medium sentences. No padding. No hedging. Every sentence earns its place.
Rhythm: statement, development, conclusion. Not: context-setting, qualification, caveat, eventual point.

### Pacing
Deliberate. Not breathless. Not staccato. There is a difference between punchy and abrupt — LucentSignal copy should be the former, never the latter.

### Emotional tone
Calm confidence. Not cheerful. Not urgent. Not clever. The tone of a senior professional who knows what they are talking about and does not need to perform it.

### Sophistication level
Assumes intelligence. Does not over-explain. Does not use jargon to sound smart or plain language to sound accessible — uses the right words for the idea.

### Confidence level
High. LucentSignal copy does not hedge, qualify, or seek approval. It states things plainly and lets the user decide.

### Restraint principles

- One idea per sentence
- One idea per paragraph in longer copy
- If a word can be removed without changing meaning, remove it
- Never use two adjectives where one will do
- Never use an adverb where a stronger verb will do

### Humour boundaries
Dry, understated, and rare. Never self-deprecating about the product. Never forced. If the copy is trying to be funny, it is wrong.

### Good copy examples

- CTA: "Write my first post →"
- CTA: "Continue refining this post →"
- CTA: "Try it on your own idea →"
- Loading state: "Exploring angles…"
- Loading state: "Building a stronger hook…"
- Feature label: "Your voice profile"
- Trust line: "For professionals who already have something worth saying."
- Free trial frame: "Try the full workflow free — no card needed."
- Upgrade prompt: "This feature is available on Creator and Pro plans."

### Bad copy examples

- CTA: "Start free" — generic, says nothing
- CTA: "Try it free" — vague, no momentum
- CTA: "Get started" — meaningless
- Loading state: "Our AI is working its magic…" — gimmicky
- Feature label: "AI Voice Matcher Pro" — gimmicky, overcapitalised
- Trust line: "Join thousands of creators" — no users yet, and "creators" is the wrong word
- Upgrade prompt: "🔒 UPGRADE NOW to unlock this feature!" — aggressive, cheap
- Headline: "Supercharge your LinkedIn presence" — banned phrase
- Subhead: "Leverage AI to transform your content strategy" — three banned concepts in one sentence

### Permanently banned words and phrases
These are never acceptable in any LucentSignal copy — product UI, marketing site, emails, loading states, error messages, or anywhere else.

**Banned words:**
supercharge, unlock (as metaphor for upgrade), leverage (as verb), empower, transformative, revolutionary, game-changer, cutting-edge, seamless, robust, innovative, next-level, world-class, best-in-class, streamline, synergy, holistic, deep dive, circle back, unpack, move the needle, 10x, skyrocket

**Banned phrases:**
- "AI-powered [anything]"
- "Transform your [anything]"
- "Unlock your potential"
- "Take your [anything] to the next level"
- "The [anything] you deserve"
- "Join [number] users"
- "Our powerful AI"
- "Magic" in any product context
- "In seconds" as a primary claim
- "Content that converts"
- "Grow your audience"
- "Build your personal brand" (acceptable only in very specific contexts, not as a headline)

## 5. UX Writing Principles

### CTA philosophy
Every CTA must answer: what happens when I click this?
"Start free" answers nothing. "Write my first post" answers everything.
CTAs should be action-specific, outcome-oriented, and progressive. The CTA the user sees at the bottom of the page should be meaningfully different from the one at the top — it should reflect where they are in the journey, not repeat the same ask.

**CTA hierarchy:**
- Soft CTA: curiosity, no commitment — "See how it works"
- Medium CTA: light commitment — "Try it on your own idea"
- Hard CTA: signup intent — "Write my first post, free →"
- Post-generation CTA: maximum investment — "Continue refining this post →"

### Onboarding copy rules

- Never welcome the user — they are already here
- Never explain what to do next if the interface makes it obvious
- Never use instructional language if the UI affords the action directly
- Progress indicators should show where the user is, not what the system is doing
- Empty states should suggest the next action, not describe the absence of content

### Loading state behaviour
Loading states are not dead time. They are an opportunity to communicate intelligence and process.
Rules:

- Rotate between 3–5 short messages
- Each message describes a real stage of the process
- Messages should sound like a skilled editor's internal monologue
- Never use progress bars unless the time is genuinely measurable
- Never use spinning logos or animation theatre
- Maximum message length: 5 words

**Approved loading messages:**
- "Exploring angles…"
- "Building a stronger hook…"
- "Looking for a sharper point of view…"
- "Shaping the argument…"
- "Finding the right opening…"
- "Checking the structure…"
- "Tightening the close…"

**Never use:**
- "Please wait…"
- "Our AI is working…"
- "This may take a moment…"
- "Generating your content…"
- Percentage bars for AI generation

### Guest mode language
Guest mode language must never feel like a sales pitch. It must feel like honest product communication.
Rules:

- Never say "upgrade" to a guest — they have not paid for anything yet
- Never use "unlock" as a verb in guest mode
- Show what is locked without making the user feel punished for not paying
- The locked state should feel like: "this is here when you need it" — not "you cannot have this"
- Badge language: "Creator plan" or "Pro plan" — not "PREMIUM" or "PRO ONLY"

### Upgrade language
Upgrade prompts must feel like a natural continuation, not an interruption.
Rules:

- Never use modal overlays for upgrade prompts unless the user has definitively hit a hard limit
- Locked features should show plan requirements inline — not in a popup
- Upgrade prompts should describe the feature, not the plan
- The primary message is always what the feature does — the plan requirement is secondary

Correct: "Schedule to calendar — available on Creator and Pro plans"
Incorrect: "⚠️ UPGRADE REQUIRED — This is a Pro feature"

### Scoring language
Scoring is a quality signal, not a grade. The language must reflect this.

- "Your post scored 34/40" — not "Your score is 34"
- "Hook could be sharper" — not "Hook: weak"
- "Insight is strong but slightly broad" — not "Insight: 8/10 — good"
- Dimension names: Hook, Insight, Clarity, Closing — always these four, always in this order

### Dashboard empty states
Empty states should not describe what is missing. They should suggest what is possible.
- Correct: "Your posts will appear here once you start writing."
- Incorrect: "You have no posts yet."

### Error messaging

- Never blame the user
- Never use technical language
- Always suggest a next action
- Keep it to one sentence where possible

Correct: "Something went wrong with scoring — try again and it will run automatically."
Incorrect: "Error 503: Scoring service unavailable."

### Confirmation messages

- Confirm the action, not the system state
- Use past tense for completed actions
- One sentence, no exclamation marks

Correct: "Your post was saved."
Incorrect: "Success! Your post has been saved to your library! 🎉"

## 6. Behavioural Conversion System

### Activation psychology
The user must feel invested before they are asked to sign up. Investment comes from participation, not from reading about the product.
The activation bridge — where the user types their own idea on the landing page — is the most important conversion mechanism in the product. It creates psychological ownership of a piece of content before the user has committed to anything.
Sequence: curiosity → participation → investment → commitment → signup
This sequence cannot be shortcut.

### Commitment escalation
Each step in the user journey should ask for slightly more than the last — but never too much at once.

1. Landing page: read about the product (zero commitment)
2. Activation bridge: type a topic (micro-commitment)
3. App: generate a post (small commitment)
4. Post output: see the score and locked features (investment)
5. Signup prompt: "Continue refining this post" (high intent, clear payoff)

### Workflow framing
The product is always framed as a workflow in progress — not a completed output.
The generated post should never feel finished. The score panel, the improvement suggestions, and the locked refinement tools should create a clear sense of: "There is more to do here, and I want to do it."

### Behavioural sequencing
The guest experience should follow this sequence:

1. User arrives — sees the generator (not a dashboard, not a signup wall)
2. User types their topic — activates psychological ownership
3. User generates — sees their own idea in draft form
4. User sees the score — understands there is a quality standard
5. User sees locked features — understands there is a larger system
6. User sees the CTA — "Continue refining this post" — makes sense, has payoff

Each step must happen before the next. Do not skip steps.

### Curiosity loops
The scoring system creates a curiosity loop: the user wants to know if their post is good, they get a partial answer (the score), and they can see how to make it better — but refinement requires signing up.
This is the product's most powerful conversion mechanism after the activation bridge.

### Premium conversion mechanics
Premium users convert when they believe the product will make their output meaningfully better than what they could do themselves.
The proof is in the scoring. A post that scores 28/40 with specific, accurate improvement suggestions is more convincing than any marketing copy.

### Trust reinforcement
Trust is built through:

- Output quality — the generated post sounds like a real person, not a template
- Scoring accuracy — the score reflects genuine quality dimensions, not arbitrary numbers
- Visual restraint — the product looks like it was built by people who take quality seriously
- Copy discipline — the product communicates the way it expects its users to communicate

Trust is destroyed by:

- Generic AI output
- Aggressive upgrade prompts
- Cheap modal overlays
- Inconsistent naming
- Copy that uses the words the product is supposed to help users avoid

### Why users convert
Users convert when the generated post is good enough that they want to keep working on it, and they can clearly see what keeping working on it looks like.
The conversion moment is emotional, not rational. It is: "I want this post to be right, and I can see how to get it there."

### Why users abandon
Users abandon when:

- The generated post sounds generic or templated
- The upgrade wall appears before they have invested in anything
- The product feels like every other AI tool
- The signup friction is higher than the perceived payoff
- The experience feels transactional — one post, done, nothing more to do

## 7. Marketing Site ↔ Product Alignment Rules

### The principle
A user who reads the landing page and then opens the app should feel they have walked into the same building through a different door — not that they have left one product and entered another.

### What must match

- Background colour: #0B0F1A
- Green accent: #00E676
- Font: Poppins
- Card background: #151B2E
- Card border: rgba(255,255,255,0.08)
- Header structure: LucentSignal wordmark left, CTA right
- Footer structure: brand credit left, links right
- Logo SVG: identical between marketing site and app guest mode
- Wordmark style: "Lucent" white, "Signal" green — no exceptions

### What must never happen

- Marketing site and app using different plan names
- Marketing site and app showing different prices
- Marketing site CTAs pointing to Railway URLs
- App guest mode using a different header or footer structure to the marketing site
- App guest mode using different CTA language to the marketing site
- Any element in the app that would look out of place on the marketing site, or vice versa

### Typography consistency

- Headlines: Poppins 600
- Body: Poppins 400
- Labels/badges: Poppins 600, uppercase, letter-spacing 0.07em
- Microcopy: Poppins 400, opacity 0.4–0.5

### Visual restraint principles

- No gradients unless they were in the original design system
- No drop shadows heavier than rgba(0,0,0,0.3)
- No border-radius above 16px on cards, 24px on modals
- No bright colours other than #00E676 and #FFB300 (amber for warnings)
- No icons larger than 24px in body content, 32px in feature cards
- No full-width green buttons — use max-width or contained layout

### Dark theme principles

- Background is always #0B0F1A — never pure black, never mid-grey
- Cards are #151B2E — never white, never light
- Text hierarchy: white (primary), rgba(255,255,255,0.65) (secondary), rgba(255,255,255,0.35) (tertiary), rgba(255,255,255,0.2) (disabled/placeholder)
- Green is for active states, CTAs, and success indicators only — not decoration
- Amber (#FFB300) is for warnings and quota alerts only

## 8. Guest Mode Philosophy

### What guest mode exists to do
Guest mode exists to convert a visitor into a signed-up user by giving them a genuine, valuable experience of the product before asking for any commitment.
It is not a demo. It is not a lead capture form. It is the beginning of the actual workflow — and the user should feel that.

### The emotional journey

1. Curiosity — the user arrives wanting to see what the product does
2. Participation — they type their own idea and generate a real draft
3. Investment — they see their draft, the score, and the improvement possibilities
4. Desire — they want to keep working on this specific post
5. Conversion — signing up is the natural next step, not an interruption

### What should remain visible

- The full generator interface including industry selector, topic input, post style, and writing style
- Locked features with clear labels and plan requirements — visible but inactive
- Platform export icons — visible but inactive
- The scoring panel after generation — partially revealed
- The workflow panel showing what comes next — visible but locked

### What should remain locked

- Trending topics functionality (visible but inactive)
- Voice profile upload (visible but inactive)
- Schedule to calendar (visible but inactive)
- Export to platforms (visible but inactive)
- Refinement tools — sharper, clearer, tighter (locked post-generation)
- Save to content library (locked post-generation)
- Repurpose into multiple versions (locked post-generation)

### How friction should work
Friction in guest mode should feel like: "I want to do more but I need to create an account to continue." Not like: "I have been blocked."
The locked features must look desirable, not forbidden. The signup CTA must feel like a continuation, not an interruption.

### How conversion should happen naturally

1. User generates a post
2. User sees "Your draft" framing — the post feels curated, not dumped
3. User sees the score — partial quality signal
4. User sees improvement suggestions — the post is not finished
5. User sees locked refinement tools — the continuation is clear
6. CTA appears: "Continue refining this post →" — natural next step

### What should never appear in guest mode

- Countdown timers or urgency mechanics
- "LIMITED TIME OFFER" language
- Percentage discount popups
- Red warning banners
- "You have used X of Y free posts" counters that feel punitive
- Aggressive modal overlays on first visit
- Any language that implies the user is being tracked or limited

### What would cheapen the experience

- Generic SaaS upgrade walls
- Bright orange "UPGRADE NOW" buttons
- Lock icons that dominate the UI
- Feature cards that look disabled rather than reserved
- Copy that says "unlock" as though features are being withheld
- Any pattern that makes the product feel like a freemium trap

## 9. Feature Naming System

### Naming philosophy
Feature names should describe what the feature does in plain language. They should not be branded, AI-inflected, or clever.
A feature name is not a marketing opportunity. It is a navigational label. Clarity wins over creativity.

### Naming conventions

- Two to three words maximum
- Title case for primary feature names
- Sentence case for descriptions and labels
- No product suffixes (not "Voice Pro" or "AI Tone Matcher")
- No version numbers in feature names (not "Scoring 2.0")
- No action verbs in feature names unless the feature is a single action ("Schedule", "Export")

### Correct naming examples

- "Your voice profile" — not "AI Voice Cloner" or "Brand Voice Pro"
- "Trending topics" — not "AI Topic Discovery Engine"
- "Post scoring" — not "Quality AI Score" or "Content Rater"
- "Tone and tension" — not "Emotional Impact Dial" or "Vibe Settings"
- "Content calendar" — not "Publishing Command Centre"
- "Repurpose" — not "Content Multiplier AI"
- "Ghost profile" — not "Personal Brand Voice DNA"

### Prohibited naming styles

- Anything with "AI" as an adjective: "AI Writer", "AI Coach", "AI Insights"
- Anything with "Pro" as a suffix used generically: "Analytics Pro", "Export Pro"
- Anything with "Smart": "SmartTone", "SmartSchedule"
- Anything with "Power": "PowerEdit", "PowerVoice"
- Anything that sounds like a feature in a productivity app from 2015
- Anything that a competitor already uses as a primary brand term

## 10. Pricing and Upgrade Psychology

### How pricing should feel
Pricing should feel like a fair exchange for real value — not a barrier, not a bargain, not a pressure moment.
The user should arrive at the pricing section having already experienced enough of the product to know what they are paying for.

### How upgrades should appear
Rules:

- Never interrupt a user mid-task with an upgrade prompt
- Always show the upgrade requirement inline with the locked feature — not in a separate popup
- Upgrade prompts should describe the benefit, not the plan
- The plan name is secondary to what the feature does

### How plans should be differentiated

- **Free**: 3 posts to experience the full workflow. No calendar, no export, no voice profile. Enough to know if the product is worth paying for.
- **Creator (£19/month, 20 posts)**: The full individual workflow. Calendar, export, trending topics. For professionals publishing consistently.
- **Pro (£39/month, 75 posts)**: Everything in Creator plus voice profile and higher volume. For power users and agency operators.

### How free trials should be framed
The free tier is not a "trial." It is a genuine experience of the product's core workflow.
- Correct framing: "Try the full workflow free — no card needed."
- Incorrect framing: "Start your free trial" (implies it expires and they lose something)

### What creates premium trust

- Output quality that surprises the user
- Scoring that feels accurate and professional
- Refinement suggestions that are specific, not generic
- A product that looks and feels like it was built by people with taste
- Copy that treats the user as intelligent
- No dark patterns, no urgency tricks, no fake social proof

### What destroys trust

- Generated output that sounds like AI
- Aggressive upgrade modals at the wrong moment
- Inconsistent pricing between the marketing site and the app
- Plan names that do not match between contexts
- Railway URLs in marketing copy
- Any element that makes the product feel hastily built

## 11. Product Philosophy

### Permanent product principles

**Quality over volume.** One excellent post is worth more than ten mediocre ones. Every product decision should reinforce the quality standard, not undermine it.

**Workflow over generation.** The generated draft is not the product. The workflow from rough idea to publish-ready post is the product. Every feature should serve the workflow.

**Confidence is the outcome.** The user's emotional destination is confidence that what they are about to publish represents them well. Every product decision should build toward that state.

**Restraint is a feature.** The product does less than it could, on purpose. Every feature that is not in the core workflow creates noise. Noise destroys trust with this audience.

**The user is already good.** LucentSignal does not make users better writers. It removes the friction between what they already know and what they can publish. The product is in service of the user's existing quality — not a replacement for it.

### Behavioural principles

- Never ask for commitment before delivering value
- Never show a paywall before the user has invested in something
- Never interrupt a task to ask for an upgrade
- Always show what is possible before revealing what is locked
- Always make the next step obvious without making it feel forced

### UX commandments

- The interface should communicate before the user reads a word
- Every element earns its place or it is removed
- Empty states are not failure states — they are invitation states
- Locked features must look desirable, not disabled
- Loading states must feel intelligent, not mechanical
- Error messages must suggest a path forward, not assign blame
- Upgrade prompts must feel like opportunities, not punishments
- The guest experience must feel like the beginning of something, not the end

### Anti-patterns — things LucentSignal must never become

- A content volume machine — the product is about quality, not output rate
- A growth hacking tool — the audience actively distrusts this positioning
- A generic AI wrapper — the product's value is the workflow, not the model
- A dashboard with no purpose — every screen must serve the workflow
- A feature catalogue — complexity is the enemy of trust with this audience
- A product that talks like a startup — the tone must match the audience's professional standard
- A free tool with a paywall — the free experience must be genuinely valuable
- A product that embarrasses its users — if the output is not good enough to publish, the product has failed

### Things that would be unforgivable

- Generated posts that sound like AI
- Scoring that feels random or inaccurate
- Pricing inconsistency between marketing site and app
- Copy that uses any of the banned phrases
- A guest experience that feels like a trap
- An upgrade prompt that makes a user feel punished
- An interface that a senior professional would be embarrassed to be seen using
