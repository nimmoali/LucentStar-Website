Your idea AI is changing hiring decisions, and experience is starting to matter more than credentials
Measured: explores the tension without landing a verdict

There's a shift happening in how hiring decisions are made, but it's easy to miss if you're not looking closely.

For a long time, credentials acted as a proxy for capability. They offered reassurance: a signal that someone had met a certain standard. That still matters. But it seems to matter less than it used to.

As AI takes on more structured work, the emphasis moves elsewhere. Judgement becomes more important. So does the ability to navigate ambiguity and make decisions without a clear template.

Those qualities are harder to validate on paper. They tend to show up through experience, through exposure to real situations where the outcome wasn't obvious from the start.

What's less clear is whether most hiring processes are built to surface that. The signals being used were designed for a different kind of work. Whether they're being updated at the same pace as the work itself is a harder question.

Direct: clear position, no hedging

We've treated hiring like a credentials problem for years. It isn't. It's a risk problem.

Degrees and certifications gave companies a way to feel confident about decisions. A way to reduce uncertainty. But they never really proved someone could do the work.

AI is starting to make that obvious.

When structured tasks are handled by systems, the value shifts to judgement. To knowing what matters when the brief isn't clear. To making decisions without a playbook. You don't get that from a certificate. You get it from experience, from seeing things go wrong and understanding why.

The issue is that most hiring processes haven't caught up. They're still built around signals that are becoming less relevant. That creates a gap between people who look qualified and people who actually are.

That gap is only going to widen.

#Hiring #AI #Leadership #FutureOfWork
Sharp: challenges assumptions, creates friction

We're still hiring for a version of work that doesn't exist anymore.

Credentials made sense when the job was predictable. When success meant following a known process and getting the same outcome every time. That's not the environment most teams are operating in now.

AI is already handling the structured work. The parts that used to justify those credentials in the first place.

What's left is the messy part. The judgement calls. The situations where there isn't a clear answer. And that's exactly where credentials tell you the least. They don't show how someone thinks. They don't show how they handle ambiguity. They don't show how they recover when things go wrong.

Yet we still treat them as the primary filter. If hiring doesn't evolve, companies won't just miss good candidates. They'll systematically select the wrong ones.
